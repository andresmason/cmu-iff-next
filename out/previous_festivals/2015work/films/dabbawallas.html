The Dabbawallas

Directed by Paul Goodman | 2004 | India/USA | 60 min.



It is a business that has thrived for the past 100 years, but you will not find an app for that, or an online order form, or any database behind the system. You probably never will. Each morning, some 5,000 Dabbawallas zigzag through the city, negotiating the maze of streets teeming with motor and foot traffic. These "box people" collect hot lunches from worker's homes, and deliver each meal to its intended recipient before the clock strikes noon. Using an elaborate system of colour-coded boxes to convey over 170,000 meals to their destinations each day, the Dabbawalla collective has built up an extraordinary reputation for the speed and accuracy of its deliveries. Word of their legendary efficiency and almost flawless logistics spread through the rarefied world of management consulting. Harvard Business School produced a case study of the Dabbawallas, urging its students to learn from the organisation, which relies entirely on human endeavour and employs no technology. The Dabbawallas, who all receive the same pay, are also seen as paragons of "bottom up" social entrepreneurship. C.K. Prahalad, a professor at the University of Michigan's Ross School of Business, says they show how a home-grown business can help lift workers at the “bottom of the pyramid” out of poverty. They also contradict the stereotype of developing-world labourers as low-wage economic victims.

CMU's own Paul Goodman, as professor of organizational psychology at the Tepper School of Business, made this 2003 documentary on the Dabbawallas. Goodman explained the appeal of this system to Western management thinkers. "Most of our modern business education is about analytic models, technology and efficient business practices. The Dabbawallas, by contrast, focuses more on human and social ingenuity," he said.

Perhaps you will also want to see the Indian feature film, The Lunchbox (2013), based on the workings of this extraordinary, everyday institution in Mumbai. Free-market capitalism at its finest!

Director

Paul S. Goodman, who was Director of the Center for theManagement of Technology at CMU, used video as a powerful tool to help students understand the changing nature of work. At CMU, he produced 20 educational videos, as well as two documentaries (including The Dabbawallas) which were aired on PBS stations nationwide. Shipbreakers, his third and last documentary, focused on the business of “ship breaking” old cargo freighters in Vietnam, China and India, and premiered at last year’s CMU International Film Festival.
