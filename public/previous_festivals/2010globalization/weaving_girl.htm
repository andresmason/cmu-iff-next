Directed by Wang Quanan; China, 2009; 90 min

Introduction and post-film discussion moderated by Dr. Xinmin Liu, Professor of East Asian Languages and Literatures and Film Studies at the University of Pittsburgh.

American Premiere

"Although it initially seems to be an indictment of working conditions in China, the film ends up telling a personal story that, although not as politically incisive, will strike a more universal chord for audiences around the globe.”
-Darcy Paquet, Screen Daily

Awards

2009 Montreal World Film Festival- Jury’s Selection- Grand Prix
2009 International Film Critics- FIPRESCI Prize
Two textile workers smoke their cigarettes during a brief break from the reality of reduced lunch breaks, docked pay and chemicals.  The older of the two turns to the younger woman who puffs her cigarette with a distant and distracted look in her eyes.  “I was sick once too,” she says, “You need to take time for yourself, spend some money on yourself, go dancing for you.”  The younger woman pushes past the revelation of her incurable disease and listens to the accordion music of the factory choir's musician.  The music rekindles the memory of a former lover that she had buried under the starker realities of motherhood, meeting end’s meet and illness.  With what remains of her life, she decides to see what remains of her love.

Wang Quan'an

Born in Yan'an, in the Shaanxi Province of China in 1965, Wang Quan'an attended film school in Beijing.  He joined the Xian Film Studio after his graduation in 1991, writing screenplays between assignments as a producer.  He made his directorial debut in 1999 with Lunar Eclipse, which won awards at film festivals in Russia and France.  His subsequent films, The Story of Ermei (2004) and Tuya’s Marriage (2006), were also widely shown on the international festival circuit, the latter winning the Golden Bear at the Berlin Festival.
