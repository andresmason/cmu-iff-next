An ex-soldier faces the reality of a revenge killing operation.  His girlfriend faces the reality that she may love a murderer.  Z32 is the dialogue that confronts what has not been confronted between them.  This soldier could be faceless- any soldier, from any war, dealing with the consequences of what he was told to do and what he did.  He needed to be faceless for the protection anonymity brings.  Director Avi Mograbi turns what could have been a filmmaking obstacle into a constructive force, experimenting with editing to hide the face of one soldier for the sake of revealing the faces of global soldiers.

Featuring panel discussion from:

Dr. Naftali Kaminski, an Israeli physician scientist residing in Pittsburgh, is a renowned expert in lung fibrosis and a leader lung disease research. Dr Kaminski has authored more than a hundred scientific papers and book chapters. Additionally, Dr Kaminski has been active for peace and reconciliation between Israelis and Palestinians and is a member of the Middle East Peace Forum of Pittsburgh and a founding member of J Street Pittsburgh.

Helen Gerhardt served in Iraq with the 1221st Missouri Army National Guard transportation company from June of 2003 to July of 2004. She is currently pursuing a MFA in the Creative Nonfiction Writing Program at the University of Pittsburgh. She is writing a book about her company’s deployment to Iraq, the ongoing consequences of the War on Terror and stories of Iraqi refugees. Her essays have been featured in the New York Times.

Barry Fisher is a full time psychiatrist who specializes in treating combat veterans. He is the chair of the Pennsylvania Psychiatric Society Committee on VA and State Hospital Issues. He has treated veterans of WWII, Korea, Vietnam, the Cold War, the Gulf War and now Iraq and Afghanistan.

Barry Paris is a film reviewer for the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette, and co-host of a weekly radio show on WQED-FM. His best-known works include acclaimed biographies of film stars Louise Brooks, Greta Garbo and Audrey Hepburn. Paris has won multiple awards for cultural and investigative reporting.

Eran Toch is 35 year-old post-doctoral fellow at the School of Computer Science, Carnegie Mellon University. He served as a soldier and non-commissioned officer in the NAHAL infantry brigade as part of his mandatory army service, and now serves in the IDF reserves.



Avi Mograbi

Mograbi has been called the Michael Moore of Israel. Born and educated in Israel, Mograbi studied philosophy and art before pursuing a career in film. A long-time peace activist, Mograbi was jailed in 1982, when he refused to serve in Lebanon. For the past eighteen years, he has worked in the Israeli feature and commercial film industry as an assistant director, screenwriter, production manager and director.  Among his other feature documentaries are How I Learned to Overcome My Fear and Love Arik Sharon (1997), Happy Birthday, Mr. Mograbi (1999) and August, which won the Peace Award at the 2002 Berlin Film Festival.
